Understanding Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium in Population Genetics

Dr. Sarah Chen December 1, 2024

The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium is a fundamental principle in population genetics that describes the relationship between allele and genotype frequencies in an idealized population.

Mathematical Foundation

For a gene with two alleles A and a, with frequencies p and q respectively, where p + q = 1, the Hardy-Weinberg principle states that genotype frequencies will be:

$$P(AA) = p^2$$ $$P(Aa) = 2pq$$ $$P(aa) = q^2$$

The sum of these frequencies equals 1:

$$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = (p + q)^2 = 1$$

Chi-square Test for HWE

To test whether observed genotype frequencies deviate from Hardy-Weinberg expectations, we use the chi-square test:

$$\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}$$

Where \(O_i\) represents observed frequencies and \(E_i\) represents expected frequencies under HWE.

Applications in Statistical Genetics

Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium testing is crucial in quality control for genome-wide association studies (GWAS). Significant deviations from HWE may indicate:

  • Genotyping errors
  • Population stratification
  • Inbreeding
  • Natural selection
